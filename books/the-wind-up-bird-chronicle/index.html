The Wind-Up Bird Chronicle

by Haruki Murakami

A "dreamlike and compelling” tour de force (Chicago Tribune)—an astonishingly imaginative detective story, an account of a disintegrating marriage, and an excavation of the buried secrets from Japan’s forgotten campaign in Manchuria during World War II. Now with a new introduction by the author. In a Tokyo suburb, a young man named Toru Okada searches for his wife’s missing cat—and then for his wife as well—in a netherworld beneath the city’s placid surface. As these searches intersect, he encounters a bizarre group of allies and antagonists. Gripping, prophetic, and suffused with comedy and menace, this is one of Haruki Murakami’s most acclaimed and beloved novels.

What Readers Say

Readers noted that this book required them to stop reading for a couple of days to take it all in, indicating its dense and emotionally taxing nature. It is praised for making readers think about things they've never thought before, depending on the timing of when they read it.

Readers described this book as perfect for someone in a "mundane slow moving period of life" or dealing with a breakup. One user related deeply to the protagonist sitting around waiting for life to happen after graduation, finding the character's dilemmas apt for that stage of life. However, the reception is polarized; one user physically threw the book across the room out of "boredom, confusion and frustration." Another user noted that while it's amazing for that specific life stage, they couldn't relate to the character's dilemmas because they were too busy with daily tasks. A specific criticism mentioned is that the translator cut out 7 chapters, though it is still considered Murakami's "2nd best book" by some.

This recommendation was met with significant nuance and debate. Supporters described it as sitting "somewhere between finding the romance/beauty of everyday life and pulling the absurdity out of what we often thoughtlessly consider everyday." They noted it shares a "dream-like quality" with Miyazaki films but acknowledged it is "a bit darker" and "less of the feel goodness." Critics, however, argued it is not a true match because Murakami's protagonist is often "disconnected from life and experiences it as an observer," leading to a feeling of "dissociating" rather than the mindful connection found in Miyazaki's work. Specific criticisms included the presence of "graphic sex" which some found "off putting," the story being "really slow," and the portrayal of female characters as "terrible" and the overall vibe as "dark and depressing and isolating." Despite the warnings, some readers found it "really liked" and noted the speed of the plot is "reminiscent of some ghibli movies."

Readers felt this book "felt very much a mystery" and was not focused around a murder. It is described as "magic realism" with resolutions that are "never clear or obvious." Despite the ambiguity, it compels readers to keep reading to get to the bottom of the mystery. One commenter added a heads-up about "intense description of death/being left to die."
